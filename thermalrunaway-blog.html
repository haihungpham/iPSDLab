Research Breakthrough Battery Safety

We didn’t start the fire: Battery thermal runaway chemistry simulation.

Words by Liam McManus
Feb 09, 2026

Figure 1: Complex CFD modeling of thermal propagation in high-energy density cells.

The worst-case scenario for any electric vehicle (EV) manufacturer is the uncontrolled release of energy within a battery pack. While rare, thermal runaway represents a critical safety threshold that defines the success of modern electrification.

Ensuring battery safety is a multiscale challenge. From the fundamental chemistry occurring at the electrolyte-electrode interface to the macroscopic venting of flammable gases within a pack, engineers must account for every joule. Traditionally, this has relied on physical "burn tests"—expensive, destructive, and late-stage procedures.

Thermal Runaway: A Recap

Thermal runaway is a dangerous phenomenon where a battery cell overheats uncontrollably. It begins with a triggering event—overcharge, internal short, or external heating—which initiates a self-sustaining feedback loop. As temperatures rise, the solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) decomposes, followed by cathode breakdown and electrolyte combustion.

Figure 2: Real-time sensor telemetry during controlled cathode degradation.
"Ensuring a reliable, efficient and safe battery management system with model-based design is no longer optional in the UAM and EV markets."

The Devil is in the Detail: Chemistry

Inside a battery cell, we find multiple solids (anode, cathode, and separators) and a liquid electrolyte. When reactions occur, these components decompose to release flammable gases such as hydrogen and methane.

Figure 3: Simulated HMMC gas generation profile vs temperature rise.

With the latest advancements in Simcenter STAR-CCM+, i-PSD Lab is exploring the Homogeneous Multiphase Complex Chemistry (HMMC) model. This allows our researchers to explicitly model the fundamental reactions triggered during runaway. Instead of relying on simplified heat source models, we can now simulate the actual gas generation and chemical heat release in real-time.

Laboratory Benchmarks

  • 01. Cell-level detailed chemical kinetics integration.
  • 02. HMMC modeling for venting and gas phase combustion.
  • 03. Coupled thermal-fluid design for heat shield optimization.

Simulation as a Standard

Simulation is the only way to "test" these destructive events without the physical smoke and mirrors. By integrating these chemical models into the Digital Twin, we can predict safety margins before a single cell is ever manufactured.
